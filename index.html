Key Ideas

Frameworks I work from.

These aren't abstract concepts — they're the mental models I use daily, grounded in research and field-tested at scale.

Adoption ≠ Deployment
The critical failure mode in enterprise AI is measuring tool rollout rather than sustained active usage. Success requires embedding AI into existing workflows — not bolting it on as a feature. The north star metric is active weekly usage, not licence count.
The Jagged Frontier
AI capabilities are uneven and unpredictable. Models excel at surprising tasks and fail at seemingly simple ones — often convincingly. Employees need experiential literacy before they can make good judgments about what to automate. You can't read the frontier from a job description.
Tasks Before Jobs
AI reshapes tasks within roles before it displaces roles entirely. Workforce strategy must start at the task level — mapping where AI is already changing the work, then redesigning how teams operate around that reality.
The 53-Point Gap
Research shows a 53-point disconnect between C-suite confidence in AI policy clarity and frontline employee awareness. This is a management problem, not a technology problem. Solving it requires active communication infrastructure, not better tools.
The $9-to-$1 Ratio
Technology investment alone is insufficient. For every $1 spent on AI platforms and tools, organisations need roughly $9 invested in people, process redesign, and change management to capture the value. Most enterprise AI budgets are inverted.
Delegate → Review → Iterate → Own
The practical daily pattern for building genuine AI fluency. Start by delegating a task, critically review the output, iterate with the model, and eventually own a new, higher-leverage workflow. Repeated across hundreds of tasks, this is how AI capability compounds.
